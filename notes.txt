Pre-Pheromone Allocation
Apply length-proportional allocation. Without demand data, spatial equity is the primary objective. Allocate the fleet based on route distance to maintain a uniform headway across the network.$$F_i = F_{total} \times \frac{L_i}{\sum L}$$Academic Basis: Ceder (2007), Public Transit Planning and Operation. Initial frequency setting relies on vehicle cycle times to satisfy maximum headway policies and establish baseline connectivity.


Post-Pheromone Allocation
Apply the Mohring Effect via square root scaling. Pheromones represent empirical demand. Linear allocation starves low-demand transfer routes and destroys network cooperation. Square root scaling flattens the distribution curve. It subsidizes long routes while adequately serving dense corridors.
$$F_i = F_{total} \times \frac{\sqrt{\tau_i}}{\sum \sqrt{\tau}}$$
Academic Basis: Mohring (1972), Optimization and Scale Economies in Urban Bus Transportation. This mathematical principle balances fleet operating costs against aggregate passenger wait times.

1. Route Load Factor (Demand per Jeep)
This measures operator efficiency and passenger crowding. It divides the total pheromone accumulation (demand) on a route by the number of assigned jeeps.
Formula: $Load = \frac{\sum \tau_{route}}{F_{route}}$
A high value indicates overcrowding and missed revenue. A low value indicates empty vehicles and operator financial loss.

2. Estimated Headway (Distance per Jeep)
This is the primary metric for passenger wait times. Since our simulation uses a constant baseline speed, route distance is directly proportional to cycle time.
Formula: $Headway \propto \frac{\sum L_{route}}{F_{route}}$
This quantifies the spatial gap between jeeps. High headway directly degrades system connectivity by breaking transfer links.

3. Demand-Service Parity (Equity Ratio)
This compares a route's share of the global demand against its share of the total fleet.
Formula: $Parity = \frac{F_{route} / F_{total}}{\tau_{route} / \tau_{total}}$
A ratio of 1.0 means perfect proportional service. A ratio below 1.0 means the route is underserved relative to its demand, which the Mohring effect naturally causes to subsidize longer, lower-demand routes. Tracking this ensures the subsidy does not become extreme.

Here is the academic and theoretical backing for this approach:

A. Multi-Colony Information Exchange (Middendorf et al., 2002)
In pure ACO, if you have multiple populations solving a problem, they often get stuck in local optima. Middendorf et al. demonstrated in Multi-Colony Ant Algorithms that blending the pheromone matrices of different populations significantly accelerates finding the global optimum. By merging Parent A and Parent B's matrices, we are performing a multi-colony information exchange.

B. The "Belief Space" in Cultural Algorithms (Reynolds, 1994)
Robert G. Reynolds formulated Cultural Algorithms, where evolution occurs on two levels: the Population Space (the chromosomes/jeep systems) and the Belief Space (the shared cultural knowledge/pheromones). Reynolds proved that allowing the fittest individuals to update the Belief Space proportional to their success creates a massive computational shortcut. The child doesn't just inherit its parents' physical bodies (routes); it inherits their cultural memory of where the passengers are.

C. Arithmetic Crossover of Real-Valued Vectors (Michalewicz, 1992)
The specific equation I provided—scaling the matrices by the parents' fitness scores before adding them together—is a multidimensional application of Michalewicz's Arithmetic Crossover.
$$\tau_{child} = \left( \frac{f_A}{f_A + f_B} \right) \tau_A + \left( \frac{f_B}{f_A + f_B} \right) \tau_B$$
By applying this to the epigenetic layer (the pheromones), the fitter parent exerts a mathematically heavier gravitational pull on the child's initial Gen-0 fleet allocation.

1. System Trajectory Divergence (Aggregated Minimum Fréchet)
You cannot compare Route 1 in Parent A to Route 1 in Parent B, because their internal indices are arbitrary. We apply an Aggregated Minimum Fréchet Distance. For every route in System A, the algorithm scans System B for the closest geometric match. It calculates the Fréchet distance to that nearest neighbor, then averages these minimums across the network.
Academic Basis: Eiter & Mannila (1994), Computing Discrete Fréchet Distance. Used extensively in multitrajectory clustering to measure the dissimilarity of unindexed spatial datasets.

2. Epigenetic Divergence (Pheromone Mean Squared Error)
The pheromone matrix is a high-dimensional state space. To measure the difference between two Belief Spaces, we calculate the Mean Squared Error (MSE) across all graph edges.
Academic Basis: Dorigo & Stützle (2004), Ant Colony Optimization. MSE is the standard metric for tracking pheromone evaporation and matrix convergence rates between iterations.
