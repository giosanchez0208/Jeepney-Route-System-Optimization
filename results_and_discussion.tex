\chapter{Results \& Discussion}
\noindent
This chapter evaluates the performance of the proposed memetic framework, transitioning from environmental validation and algorithmic calibration on synthetic topologies to the final network optimization of the empirical Iligan City \texttt{TravelGraph}.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Environment and Demand Generation}
\noindent
To ensure the optimization relies on valid spatial and demographic data, the digital twin of Iligan City and the simulated passenger demand must first be verified.

\subsection{\texttt{CityGraph} Arterial Network Pruning}
The spatial foundation of the simulated environment requires the extraction and processing of the complete road network of Iligan City. Utilizing the geographic bounding box coordinates [8.1500,8.3300,124.1500,124.4000], raw OpenStreetMap (OSM) data was parsed to construct the foundational Layer 0 infrastructure object, referred to as the \texttt{CityGraph}. This initial extraction phase successfully constructed a comprehensive spatial graph comprising 36,866 valid topological nodes and 76,310 directed bidirectional edges (\texttt{DirEdge}).

To computationally optimize the route generation search space and reflect realistic transit operations, the \texttt{RouteGenerator} must be strictly confined to viable transport corridors
. Without this constraint, the metaheuristic would attempt to route vehicles through residential dead-ends or service alleys, leading to combinatorial explosion and operationally infeasible jeepney lines. Consequently, a Public Utility Jeepney (PUJ) arterial function was applied to evaluate and filter the full \texttt{CityGraph}. 

Out of the 76,310 total network edges, 50,286 edges (65.9\%) were classified as non-drivable and pruned from the vehicular routing space. The remaining 26,024 edges (34.1\%) were successfully assigned the \texttt{is\_drivable=True} flag, forming the filtered arterial skeleton.

As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:citygraph_comparison}, this filtering process proves the successful removal of residential dead-ends and non-traversable nodes, restricting \texttt{Jeep} agent movement to valid, navigable transit corridors
. Furthermore, the inclusion of reference landmarks ensures the accurate topological and geographical alignment of the extracted graph
. Critically, while the arterial filtering restricts vehicle routing, the pruned residential and minor roads are not deleted from the underlying \texttt{CityGraph}; they remain fully preserved to guarantee that the complete spatial network is available for modeling passenger walking access in the subsequent \texttt{TravelGraph} construction.

\subsection{\texttt{DirectDemandModel} (DDM) Topography}

To generate realistic, demand-responsive origin and destination points, the environment utilizes a \texttt{DirectDemandModel} (DDM) that avoids uniform node sampling by assigning higher selection probability to locations exhibiting significant traffic activity and structural importance. From the complete network of 36,866 \texttt{CityGraph} nodes, a representative subset of 381 pre-impute centroid nodes was queried using centrality-weighted reservoir sampling [1]. Empirical travel times for these specific coordinates were extracted from the TomTom Traffic Flow API to compute localized traffic weights, defined formally as the ratio of free-flow travel time to dynamic travel time.

Because querying every node is computationally prohibitive and prone to API throttling, an Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW) spatial interpolation utilizing the Haversine formula was applied to diffuse these discrete traffic weights across the unqueried nodes of the network. Temporal analysis of the IDW traffic weights demonstrates clear dynamic friction across discrete query windows. During the 08:00 morning peak, the spatial traffic weights exhibited a mean of 1.188 with a maximum localized friction multiplier of 3.013. Congestion measurably subsided during the 13:00 off-peak window (mean: 1.132, max: 2.547), before reaching its highest aggregate intensity during the 17:00 evening peak (mean: 1.228, max: 3.164). 

To establish the final probability surface, these interpolated traffic weights ($W_i$) were fused with structural betweenness centrality ($C_i$) to calculate the final Origin-Destination (OD) Centrality score ($P_i = W_i^{\alpha} C_i^{\beta}$). This fused OD Centrality demonstrably serves as a superior proxy for passenger flow compared to raw topological betweenness alone. By synthesizing live traffic friction with network topology, corridors that are both structurally central and empirically congested dominate the probability surface. This mechanism successfully produces a spatially coherent demand gradient rather than isolated, scattered hot spots.

Finally, while the continuous DDM surface is computed across the full 36,866-node infrastructure topography, the active sampling space for generating jeepney routes is projected strictly onto the filtered arterial subset. This \texttt{only\_drivable} constraint guarantees that the algorithm intelligently targets high-demand passenger clusters while strictly confining candidate waypoints to viable, navigable transit corridors.

\section{Architectural Validation}
\noindent
Prior to evolutionary optimization, the foundational mechanics of the multi-layer graph and the temporal physics engine are validated.

\subsection{The Three-Layer \texttt{TravelGraph} Connectivity}
\noindent
The behavioral validity of every downstream result rests on the \texttt{TravelGraph} correctly translating a physical origin--destination request into a generalized-cost journey. To verify this, each of the six inter-layer transition types the multi-layer graph admits was isolated and rendered on the arterial skeleton. Figure~\ref{fig:layer_transitions} presents this matrix: the Start-Walk (SW) and End-Walk (EW) transitions move a passenger between the pedestrian network and a boarding or alighting node; the Wait (WA) transition holds the passenger at a stop pending a vehicle; the Ride (RI) transition advances the passenger along a transit edge; the Alight (AL) transition returns the passenger to the pedestrian network; and the Transfer (TR) transition connects one transit route to another. Each transition carries exactly the calibrated cost it should---\texttt{walk\_wt} per metre on SW and EW, the fixed \texttt{wait\_wt}~$=14.44$~\ac{EIVM} on WA, \texttt{ride\_wt} per metre on RI, the fixed \texttt{transfer\_wt}~$=15.78$~\ac{EIVM} on TR, and a zero-cost structural connector on AL---so that no component of travel burden is double-counted.

\subsection{Agent-Based Temporal Simulation}
\noindent
With the static cost structure validated, the temporal engine must reproduce the dynamic, micro-level interactions that motivate an agent-based model over a static assignment. Figure~\ref{fig:temporal_snapshots} traces the simulation state at successive ticks: \texttt{Jeep} agents advance along their fixed route loops while \texttt{Passenger} agents are generated, wait, board, and ride. Two emergent behaviors confirm the engine's fidelity. First, vehicle capacity is binding: when a jeepney reaches a stop already carrying \texttt{jeep\_capacity} passengers, waiting passengers are not boarded but held in the \texttt{WAITING} state, accruing \texttt{wait\_wt} delay until a vehicle with free capacity arrives---precisely the compounding congestion effect a capacity-free static assignment cannot represent. Second, the equidistant initialization holds vehicles on the same route at a fixed headway interval $S = L_{\text{loop}}/N$ rather than letting them cluster, suppressing the early bus-bunching instability that would otherwise distort service frequency from the first tick. Together these reproduce the realized waiting and headway dynamics that the fitness function integrates into Total User Cost.

\section{Component Calibration and Verification}
\noindent
Prior to executing the framework on the full empirical data, hyperparameter calibration was conducted on a synthetic Manhattan grid topology to isolate algorithmic behavior from geographic anomalies \citep{kepaptsoglou2009transit}.

\subsection{Mohring Fleet Allocation Stability}
The square-root Mohring allocation estimates each route's demand level by sampling passenger origin--destination journeys, so the allocation must be shown to be stable: repeated sampling on a fixed route system must not swing the per-route vehicle counts. Stability is quantified by the coefficient of variation (CV) of each route's allocation across seven independent demand samples, swept over sample sizes from $500$ to $3000$ for the production configuration of $38$ routes and a fleet of $2000$ vehicles.

The mean per-route allocation CV falls from $0.21$ at $500$ samples to $0.11$ at $2000$ samples, demonstrating that the fleet split across the network is highly reproducible across independent stochastic demand draws. The maximum per-route CV---the single least-served route---declines more slowly and settles near $0.69$. This residual is a property of integer allocation on a marginally-served corridor: when a route is assigned only a handful of the $2000$ vehicles, a change of one or two vehicles between samples registers as a large \emph{relative} variation even though it is operationally immaterial. With $94\%$ of sampled journeys utilising a transit route, the low mean CV is not a false-stable artefact of walk-only demand. The production Mohring sample size is therefore fixed at $2000$, where both the mean and the worst-case CV reach their minima. For comparison, smaller systems satisfy the stricter worst-route criterion $\mathrm{CV}\le 0.5$ outright---$4$, $8$, $16$, and $32$ routes stabilise by $150$, $50$, $300$, and $450$ samples respectively---and only the dense $38$-route operating point retains a worst-route CV above the threshold while remaining systemically stable in the mean.

\subsection{Temporal Discretization ($\Delta t$)}
\textit{Discussion:} All production runs fix the simulation time step at $\Delta t = 10\text{s}$ (\texttt{seconds\_per\_tick}). The only discretization error arises from sub-tick rounding when a vehicle reaches a stop; this error is unbiased, so across the large stochastic passenger population and the extended simulation horizon, individual overshoot and undershoot are independent and average toward zero. The residual noise is therefore dominated by, and absorbed into, the stochastic demand variance the optimizer already tolerates, while a 10-second step keeps per-evaluation runtime tractable. A standalone discretization sweep is consequently unnecessary.

\subsection{Simulation Horizon and Demand-Volume Calibration}
The simulation exposes two coupled knobs that jointly govern evaluation fidelity and per-evaluation runtime: the fixed horizon \texttt{num\_ticks} at which each run is abruptly terminated, and the passenger arrival rate \texttt{spawn\_rate\_per\_hour}. Because the framework models a continuous stream of commuters truncated at a fixed horizon---mirroring the bounded operational window of a real service period---passengers still in transit at the cutoff are not discarded but scored through the underservice penalty term of the fitness function. Passenger completion fraction is therefore a diagnostic rather than a calibration target: under continuous arrivals it cannot approach unity, because a trailing cohort is always mid-journey when the clock stops. Both knobs were instead calibrated against the stability of the fitness score itself, quantified as the coefficient of variation (CV) of $F_{\text{sim}}$ across independent replications.

Figure~\ref{fig:horizon_volume_calibration}(a) plots this CV against the simulation horizon at a fixed demand level. The CV falls steeply as the horizon lengthens---a longer run spawns more passengers, so the law of large numbers suppresses sampling noise---before flattening into a low-variance regime beyond roughly 60--80 simulated minutes. The production horizon was fixed at \texttt{num\_ticks}~$=540$ (90 minutes at $\Delta t = 10$\,s), comfortably inside this stable band. The faint rise near 80--100 minutes is the residual signature of completion bimodality (passengers flipping between completed and penalized states), but it is dominated by the passenger-count effect and remains within the stable band.

Figure~\ref{fig:horizon_volume_calibration}(b) isolates the demand-volume axis, plotting the same CV against the total passenger spawn rate at a fixed horizon. Here the variance collapse is monotonic and pronounced: for the five-route production configuration the CV falls from approximately $0.076$ at $250$\,pax/hr to below the $5\%$ acceptance threshold beyond roughly $550$--$600$\,pax/hr, while denser ten-route systems satisfy the threshold by $500$\,pax/hr. The production spawn rate was therefore set to $600$\,pax/hr---the smallest rate placing the five-route configuration firmly under the $5\%$ CV ceiling---ensuring the \texttt{MemeticEngine} selects parents on genuine structural fitness rather than stochastic sampling luck, while bounding the $\mathcal{O}(N)$ per-tick cost. These curves were generated on unoptimized, randomly initialized baseline routes; the low absolute completion fractions they exhibit are a property of inefficient generation-zero topologies, not of the calibrated horizon.

\subsection{Opportunistic Riding and Individual Travel-Delay Benefit}
A positive boarding \texttt{weight\_tolerance} permits a passenger to board an acceptable earlier-arriving jeepney rather than strictly waiting for the single vehicle on their pre-computed least-cost journey. To test whether this opportunistic behaviour benefits the individual commuter, each completed passenger's realised door-to-door time $T_{\text{actual}}$ (measured directly from the simulation) is compared against an \emph{expected} free-flow time computed from their planned journey,
\[
T_{\text{expected}} = \frac{d_{\text{walk}}}{v_{\text{walk}}} + \frac{d_{\text{ride}}}{v_{\text{ride}}},
\]
where $d_{\text{walk}}$ and $d_{\text{ride}}$ are the planned walking and in-vehicle distances and $v_{\text{walk}}$, $v_{\text{ride}}$ the corresponding speeds. The travel-time delay $\Delta T = T_{\text{actual}} - T_{\text{expected}}$ therefore isolates the waiting and congestion overhead incurred beyond the idealised ride. Completed passengers are partitioned into Group~A (waited for the planned jeepney) and Group~B (boarded an earlier alternative within tolerance), and their delays are compared with a one-sided Mann--Whitney $U$ test across boarding tolerances of $0$, $14.44$, and $100$~\ac{EIVM}.

At zero tolerance no passenger ever takes an alternative, establishing the baseline (median delay $6.47$~min). Once rational transfers are permitted, opportunistic riders enjoy a significantly smaller delay: at the production tolerance of $14.44$, Group~B's median delay is $0.70$~min against Group~A's $6.71$~min ($U = 953$, $p = 0.0022$); at a tolerance of $100$, where opportunistic riding is far more common, Group~B's median delay is $4.36$~min against $6.28$~min ($U = 20{,}275$, $p = 0.037$). Both results reject the null hypothesis at $p < 0.05$, confirming that passengers who rationally board an alternative jeepney experience a statistically significant reduction in travel delay, rather than the weight-tolerance mechanism merely perturbing path choice. It should be noted that opportunistic boarding is rare at the production tolerance---roughly $0.7\%$ of completed passengers ($6$ of $895$) at $14.44$, rising to $\sim 6\%$ ($57$ passengers) at $100$---so the mechanism benefits a small but genuine minority for whom an alternative vehicle is materially faster. The underlying travel-graph cost weights were externally validated and are not re-tuned here.

\subsection{Lamarckian Operator Mechanics}
\label{sec:lamarckian_mechanics}
\noindent
The Lamarckian local-search operators restructure a route system in the direction indicated by the Proportional Demand-Service Gap. Figure~\ref{fig:lamarckian_ops} isolates each operator on the Manhattan toy grid, drawing only the single route each operator modified, with removed edges in red and added edges in green. The Spatial Attraction (Or-opt) operator transplants a route segment toward an underserved, high-demand corridor; the Redundancy Repulsion (2-opt) operator reroutes a segment away from an oversupplied corridor; and the Tortuosity Pruning operator straightens a low-utility detour by replacing it with a direct shortest path. Each operator fires only when its triggering condition is present in the gap field: attraction, in particular, activates only when a positive-gap (underserved) corridor exists, which on a sparse demonstration fleet arises in roughly one of every six sampled systems. The synthetic Manhattan grid is used deliberately here so that the structural effect of a single move is visually unambiguous, isolated from the geometric irregularity of the real arterial map.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{chap4/figures/lamarckian_operators_toy.png}
    \caption{The three Lamarckian local-search operators isolated on the Manhattan toy grid. Each row shows the single route an operator modified before (left) and after (right) the move, with removed edges in red and added edges in green: Attraction transplants service toward an underserved corridor, Repulsion reroutes away from an oversupplied one, and Tortuosity Pruning straightens a low-utility detour.}
    \label{fig:lamarckian_ops}
\end{figure}

The premise underlying these operators is that the demand-service gap is a meaningful proxy for network quality---that a system more closely matching service to demand should also score better under the full simulation. Figure~\ref{fig:gap_vs_fitness} tests this directly across independently generated toy systems, plotting each system's total disparity $D(R)=\sum_{(i,j)}|P_{ij}-S_{ij}|$ against its simulated fitness $F_{\text{sim}}$. The relationship is negative, with a Pearson correlation of $r=-0.41$: systems with lower demand-service disparity tend toward lower (better) user cost. The correlation is moderate rather than tight, which is expected, since $F_{\text{sim}}$ additionally integrates capacity, waiting, and equity effects the gap alone does not capture; but its sign and magnitude confirm that the disparity is a legitimate, if imperfect, signal for the operators to follow. Figure~\ref{fig:demand_memory_gap} makes the construction of this signal explicit on a single route system, relating the demand prior (the \ac{DDM}), the route geometry, the post-simulation pheromone memory, and the resulting demand-service gap.

\subsection{Convergence and \texttt{AdaptiveController} Orchestration}
\noindent

The best score descends in a sequence of discrete improvement events---at generations $2,3,4,5,6$ and $11$---from $663{,}842$ to $614{,}660$ before the population converges and the elite stabilises. The mutation rate escalates as the stagnation counter climbs across plateaus and resets when a new best is found, the quadratic stagnation schedule of Section~\ref{sec:adaptive} made visible.

\subsection{Topological Hub Crossover and Lineage Tracking}
\noindent
Topological Hub Crossover is designed to preserve the economies-of-density trunk of the fitter parent while grafting complementary peripheral coverage from the other. Figure~\ref{fig:hub_crossover} demonstrates the mechanism on the toy grid. The topological hub---the set of road segments carrying the strongest combined pheromone support, here the top decile of corridors by accumulated demand memory---is first identified on parent~A (left). The routes of parent~A that traverse this hub become the child's trunk; routes of parent~B that do not geometrically conflict with the established trunk (centre) are then admitted as feeders. The resulting child (right) inherits a coherent trunk-and-feeder structure rather than a fragmented edge set, its trunk drawn from the fitter parent and its feeders from the other, as annotated in the figure. The recombination respects closed-loop validity and route isolation throughout, so every child remains a structurally valid route system.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{chap4/figures/memetic_hub_crossover.png}
    \caption{Topological Hub Crossover on the toy grid. \emph{Left:} the topological hub (top-decile corridors by pheromone) highlighted on parent~A, with the trunk routes that traverse it. \emph{Centre:} the feeder routes selected from parent~B. \emph{Right:} the child, assembled from parent~A's trunk (red) and parent~B's feeders (green).}

Across the generational record maintained by the \texttt{TelemetryEngine}'s lineage log---which stores every parent--child relationship so that any final chromosome can be traced through its full ancestry---this produces the intended effect: fitter parents disproportionately transmit their core, high-pheromone trunk corridors to their offspring, conserving the economies-of-density backbone across generations while recombination continues to explore the peripheral feeder layer.

\subsection{Epigenetic Pheromone Inheritance}
\noindent
Where the crossover transmits route geometry, Epigenetic Inheritance transmits the parents' accumulated demand memory. At reproduction the child receives a fitness-weighted arithmetic blend of the two parents' pheromone matrices, $\tau^{\text{child}}_{ij} = w_A\tau^A_{ij} + w_B\tau^B_{ij}$, with the weights inverted ($w_A = C_B/(C_A+C_B)$) so that the lower-cost (fitter) parent contributes the larger share. Figure~\ref{fig:pheromone_blend} renders the two parental pheromone surfaces and the inherited blend on a shared scale: the child's surface visibly tracks the fitter parent's high-demand corridors while retaining a fractional trace of the second parent's map, preserving exploratory diversity. This warm-start equips the offspring's subsequent local search with an informed prior over which corridors are likely to reward service, rather than forcing it to rediscover the city's demand structure from a blank matrix.

\subsection{Multi-Dimensional Phenotypic Convergence}
To verify that the memetic search space is well-behaved and that the optimizer is not merely wandering on a flat fitness landscape or stalling on local plateaus, convergence was monitored across three orthogonal structural dimensions: set-theoretic edge overlap (Jaccard similarity), topological connectivity (Graph Edit Distance, or GED), and spatial demand-coverage alignment (2D Wasserstein distance), overlaid with the collapsing fitness variance $\sigma^2(F)$. 

As the global best fitness score $F_{\text{sim}}$ descends during optimization, the population-wide Jaccard similarity index increases, indicating structural consolidation onto high-pheromone transit corridors. Simultaneously, the Graph Edit Distance between candidate route systems declines, confirming that the topological configurations are converging toward a common, optimized connectivity template. The 2D Wasserstein distance similarly collapses, demonstrating that the network's spatial coverage distribution progressively matches the underlying commuter demand surface. This simultaneous convergence in both the decision space (Jaccard, GED, and Wasserstein) and the objective space ($\sigma^2(F)$) proves that the proposed hybrid GA-ACO operators successfully steer the population toward a globally consistent, high-performance network region rather than wandering stochastically.

\section{Optimized Network for Iligan City}
\noindent
With the architectural mechanics and hyperparameters calibrated, the optimization framework is applied to the empirical Iligan City network (38 routes, 2000 vehicles). Because no digitized baseline of Iligan's existing paratransit route system is available, a stochastic cohort of randomly initialized generation-zero networks was simulated under identical conditions to establish a rigorous reference baseline.

\subsection{Stochastic Baseline Generation}
The stochastic baseline establishes the unoptimized reference by evaluating a population of randomly generated route systems. Under these unoptimized configurations, the simulated passenger agents experience severe routing friction. Due to overlapping trajectories, redundant stop loops, and a lack of capacity-aware coordination, passengers face extended waiting times and frequent forced transfers. Across the unoptimized cohort, the baseline fitness converges to a mean Total User Cost of $729{,}592 \pm 33{,}000$ EIVM, with an L1 demand-service disparity index $D(\mathbf{R}) = \sum |P_{ij} - S_{ij}|$ of $0.591 \pm 0.033$. This high baseline disparity reflects the structural mismatch between uncoordinated routes and the actual spatial distribution of commuter demand.

\subsection{Equity and Distribution Analysis}
A key objective of the optimized transit design is preventing peripheral or low-density communities from being underserved due to a narrow focus on system-wide travel time averages. Evaluating the travel time distributions reveals that the optimized network significantly reduces the standard deviation of passenger travel times. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:equity_traveltime_hist}, the long tail of extreme commute times—representing commuters from outlying districts who face multiple transfers or long walking distances—is substantially compressed compared to the stochastic baseline. This compression confirms that the equity term successfully forces the optimizer to maintain accessible feeder services to peripheral areas without sacrificing overall network efficiency.

\subsection{Network Resilience and Path Diversity}
To ensure the optimized route layout does not create single bottlenecks vulnerable to localized delays or vehicle capacity shortages, passenger path diversity was evaluated using Shannon entropy. A higher Shannon entropy score indicates that passenger journeys are distributed across multiple parallel paths and transfer options rather than concentrated on a single critical corridor.

The optimized systems maintain high path-diversity scores (mean Shannon entropy of approximately 3.2 bits), showing that commuters benefit from multiple viable routing alternatives. Under micro-level congestion or capacity constraints (such as fully loaded vehicles bypassing a stop), the multi-layered graph allows passengers to seamlessly adapt by boarding alternative routes or using secondary transfers. This topological flexibility ensures that the optimized network is resilient to localized disruptions.
