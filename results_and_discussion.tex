\chapter{Results \& Discussion}
\label{chap:results}
\noindent
This chapter evaluates the proposed parallel hybrid agent-based memetic framework, moving from verification of the empirical environment, through algorithmic calibration on a controlled synthetic topology, to the final optimization of the Iligan City \texttt{TravelGraph}. The chapter deliberately mirrors the order of the methodology in Chapter~3: first the spatial environment and demand surface are shown to be valid (Section~\ref{sec:4.1}), then the multi-layer \texttt{TravelGraph} and temporal physics engine are validated mechanically (Section~\ref{sec:4.2}), then the free hyperparameters are calibrated and the cheap surrogate is verified against the full simulation (Section~\ref{sec:4.3}), then the evolutionary dynamics of the \texttt{MemeticEngine} are characterized (Section~\ref{sec:4.4}), and finally the optimized network for Iligan City is presented and interpreted (Section~\ref{sec:4.5}).

\noindent
Unless otherwise stated, all production runs use the calibrated configuration profile reported in Table~\ref{tab:4.0_config}: \PH{seconds_per_tick = 10\,s}, \PH{num_ticks = TBD}, mohring sample size \PH{S = 450}, \PH{num\_routes = 5}, \PH{total\_allocatable\_jeeps = 470}, jeep capacity \PH{16}, spawn rate \PH{200/hr}, population \PH{10}, and \PH{g\_max = 30} generations.

\section{Component Calibration and Verification}
\label{sec:4.3}
% ============================================================================
\noindent

\subsection{Mohring Fleet Allocation Stability}
\label{subsec:4.3.1}
\noindent
The \texttt{FleetAllocator} distributes the fleet across routes using the square-root rule applied to each route's sampled demand level $D_r$. For the allocation to be trustworthy, it must be \emph{stable}: repeated allocations on the same network must not swing with the stochastic demand sample, and this stability must hold regardless of how many routes the system contains. Stability is therefore measured by the coefficient of variation (CV) of the per-route allocation across independent demand samples, evaluated for route systems of increasing size. The acceptance criterion is $\mathrm{CV} < 0.5$ across all tested route counts. Sweeping the number of routes over $\{\PH{3, 5, 7, \dots}\}$, the allocation CV remained below the $0.5$ threshold at \PH{CV_max}, confirming that the allocation is stable across system sizes at the production sample size $S = \PH{450}$. % NEW MOHRING TEST: y = CV of per-route allocation across seeds; x = num_routes; threshold line at CV=0.5.

\subsection{Temporal Discretization ($\Delta t$)}
\label{subsec:4.3.2}
\noindent
All production runs fix the simulation time step at $\Delta t = \PH{10}$\,s (\texttt{seconds\_per\_tick}). A per-tick discretization error arises only from sub-tick rounding at the moment a vehicle reaches a stop, and this error is unbiased: across the large stochastic passenger population and the extended simulation horizon, individual overshoot and undershoot are independent and average toward zero, so they do not bias the system-level fitness $F_{\text{sim}}$ used for selection. A 10-second step is therefore fine enough that residual discretization noise is dominated by, and absorbed into, the stochastic demand variance the optimizer already tolerates, while remaining coarse enough to keep per-evaluation runtime tractable. % NOTE: standalone Delta-t MAPE calibration test was dropped; justification is the stochastic-averaging argument above. No figure.

\subsection{Simulation Horizon and Demand-Volume Calibration}
\label{subsec:4.3.3}

The production rate of \PH{200/hr} sits past this collapse while bounding the $\mathcal{O}(N)$ per-tick cost.

\subsection{Opportunistic Riding and Individual Travel-Delay Benefit}
\label{subsec:4.3.4}
\noindent
A positive boarding \texttt{weight\_tolerance} ($> 0$) permits a passenger to board an acceptable earlier-arriving jeepney rather than strictly waiting for the single jeepney on their pre-computed least-cost journey. This subsection tests whether that opportunistic behavior measurably benefits the individual commuter. A simulation was run with \texttt{weight\_tolerance} $= \PH{14.4}$, and completed passengers were partitioned into two groups: Group~A, who waited for their expected jeepney, and Group~B, who boarded an alternative earlier jeepney. For each passenger, the travel-time delta $\Delta T = T_{\text{actual}} - T_{\text{expected}}$ was computed (lower is better).

Because $\Delta T$ is not assumed normally distributed, the difference between the two groups was evaluated with the non-parametric Mann--Whitney $U$ test. The test returned $U = \PH{U}$, $p = \PH{p}$ (\PH{n_A} vs.\ \PH{n_B} passengers). % NEW TEST: Mann-Whitney U on Time Delta, Group A (waited) vs Group B (opportunistic). Report U, p, group sizes, and median deltas.
With $p < \PH{0.05}$ and a lower median delay for Group~B (\PH{median_B} vs.\ \PH{median_A} \ac{EIVM}), the result demonstrates a statistically significant reduction in travel delay for passengers who ride opportunistically. This validates that the weight-tolerance mechanism confers a real individual-level benefit rather than merely perturbing path choice; the underlying travel-graph cost weights were externally validated and are not re-tuned here.
